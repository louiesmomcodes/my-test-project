## 1. Porter's Five Forces
**Origin:** Michael Porter, Harvard Business School
**Best for:** Mature markets, structural analysis, understanding industry attractiveness

Five forces that shape competition:
- **Threat of new entrants** — How easy is it to enter this market? (capital, regulation, switching costs)
- **Bargaining power of suppliers** — How much leverage do vendors/partners have?
- **Bargaining power of buyers** — How much leverage do customers have? (concentration, alternatives)
- **Threat of substitutes** — What alternative solutions could customers switch to?
- **Rivalry among existing competitors** — How intense is direct competition? (growth rate, differentiation, exit barriers)

**Output:** Industry attractiveness score and identification of the dominant competitive force.
**Healthcare RCM application:** Strong buyer power (large health systems), high regulatory barriers to entry,
significant switching costs due to EHR integrations.

## 2. BCG Growth-Share Matrix
**Origin:** Boston Consulting Group
**Best for:** Portfolio analysis, resource allocation, identifying where to invest vs. harvest

Plots business units or products on two axes:
- **Market growth rate** (Y-axis) — how fast is the market growing?
- **Relative market share** (X-axis) — how does this player's share compare to the leader?

Quadrants:
- **Stars** — High growth, high share → invest aggressively
- **Cash Cows** — Low growth, high share → harvest, fund Stars
- **Question Marks** — High growth, low share → decide: invest or divest
- **Dogs** — Low growth, low share → exit or niche

**Output:** Portfolio view of competitors and their likely strategic posture.

## 3. McKinsey 3 Horizons
**Origin:** McKinsey & Company (Baghai, Coley, White)
**Best for:** Understanding competitor investment posture and innovation trajectory

Three horizons of competitive activity:
- **Horizon 1** — Core business: defending and extending current products
- **Horizon 2** — Emerging opportunities: scaling adjacent or new capabilities
- **Horizon 3** — Future bets: early-stage investments in future markets

**Output:** Map of where each competitor is investing — useful for predicting their
next moves and identifying underserved horizons.

## 4. Blue Ocean Strategy
**Origin:** W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne, INSEAD
**Best for:** Identifying uncontested market space, finding whitespace, differentiation strategy

**Strategy Canvas:** Plot competitors on key industry factors (price, features, service
quality, etc.) to visualize where they compete and where gaps exist.

**ERRC Grid (Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create):**
- What factors should be **eliminated** that the industry takes for granted?
- What factors should be **reduced** well below the industry standard?
- What factors should be **raised** well above the industry standard?
- What factors should be **created** that the industry has never offered?

**Output:** Whitespace map and differentiation roadmap.

## 5. Value Proposition Canvas
**Origin:** Alex Osterwalder, Strategyzer
**Best for:** Understanding how competitors serve customer needs; ICP-level analysis

Two components:
- **Customer Profile:** Jobs (functional, social, emotional), Pains, Gains
- **Value Map:** Products/services, Pain relievers, Gain creators

**Output:** Side-by-side comparison of how each competitor addresses the same customer
profile — reveals gaps and over-served areas.

---

## 6. Business Model Canvas
**Origin:** Alex Osterwalder, Strategyzer
**Best for:** Comparing business model structure across competitors

Nine building blocks:
- Value propositions, Customer segments, Channels, Customer relationships
- Revenue streams, Key resources, Key activities, Key partnerships, Cost structure

**Output:** Structured comparison of how competitors create, deliver, and capture value.
Particularly useful for pricing model and go-to-market comparison.

---

## 7. Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Competitive Lens
**Origin:** Clayton Christensen, Harvard Business School; Tony Ulwick, Strategyn
**Best for:** Understanding functional and emotional competition beyond direct product comparison

Core premise: customers "hire" products to do a job. Competition is defined by the job,
not the product category.

Key questions:
- What job is the customer hiring this product to do?
- What were they using before (including non-consumption)?
- What progress are they trying to make?
- What are the functional, social, and emotional dimensions of the job?

**Output:** Broader competitive set (including non-obvious competitors) and insight into
where the real switching decision happens.

---

## 8. Ansoff Matrix
**Origin:** Igor Ansoff
**Best for:** Understanding competitor growth strategy and predicting expansion moves

Four growth strategies plotted on market (existing/new) vs. product (existing/new):
- **Market Penetration** — existing product, existing market
- **Market Development** — existing product, new market
- **Product Development** — new product, existing market
- **Diversification** — new product, new market

**Output:** Map of where competitors are likely expanding next; useful for anticipating
competitive moves.

---

## 9. Bain's Elements of Value
**Origin:** Bain & Company (Almquist, Senior, Bloch)
**Best for:** Understanding what dimensions of value competitors are winning on

A hierarchy of 30 elements across four categories (inspired by Maslow):
- **Functional:** saves time, simplifies, reduces risk, reduces cost, makes money, integrates, connects
- **Emotional:** reduces anxiety, rewards me, fun/entertaining, attractiveness, wellness, therapeutic
- **Life changing:** provides hope, self-actualization, motivation, heirloom, affiliation/belonging
- **Social impact:** self-transcendence

**Output:** Value element map across competitors — shows where value is concentrated
and where it is absent. Particularly useful for enterprise healthcare software where
risk reduction and integration are dominant elements.

---

## 10. Competitive Positioning Map (Perceptual Map)
**Origin:** Marketing academia; widely used by McKinsey, Bain in GTM strategy
**Best for:** Visual representation of competitive positioning on two key dimensions

Choose two dimensions that matter most to the ICP (e.g., ease of implementation vs.
depth of automation, or cost vs. breadth of coverage). Plot each competitor.

**Output:** Visual map showing clustering, whitespace, and positioning gaps.
Particularly useful for communicating competitive landscape to executives.

## 11. AI Moat Framework (AI-Era Competitive Analysis)
**Origin:** Synthesized from Bessemer Venture Partners, a16z, and practitioner writing
(2023–2026) on AI startup defensibility
**Best for:** Evaluating competitive position of AI-native companies; assessing durability
of AI-era differentiation; identifying which competitors are building lasting moats vs.
fragile wrappers

### The Core Shift
Foundation models have flattened the playing field — virtually anyone can build on top of
GPT-4 or Claude via API. This means the model itself is no longer the differentiator.
The era of the generic AI SaaS startup is effectively over; the capital and competitive
dynamics now strictly favor entities with structural, architectural defensibility.

The pattern mirrors other technology transitions: as lower layers of the stack become
commoditized, value moves up to the application layer and adjacent capabilities. In the
age of LLM applications, moats are built through specialization, proprietary data, and
deep workflow integration — not through model access.

### Five Dimensions of AI-Era Moat

**1. Model Layer Posture**
Rate each competitor: Thin wrapper / Tuned / Proprietary

- **Thin wrapper** — product is essentially an API call to a foundation model with a UI on top.
  Highly vulnerable to commoditization as foundation model providers add native features,
  or as cheaper open-source models replicate capabilities.
- **Tuned** — foundation model fine-tuned on domain-specific data, or RAG architecture with
  proprietary retrieval layer. Moderate defensibility depending on data uniqueness.
- **Proprietary** — custom-trained models on exclusive datasets, or vertically integrated
  inference infrastructure. Highest defensibility but significant capital requirement.

**2. Data Moat**
Proprietary data is the most durable AI competitive advantage. The test: is the data
valuable, unique to this company, and irreplicable by competitors? Every customer interaction
and transaction is potentially valuable training data — but only if captured systematically.

Evaluate:
- Does this competitor have exclusive access to data others cannot obtain?
- Is production usage feeding back into model improvement (active flywheel)?
- Is the data advantage growing over time, or static?

Rate: Weak / Moderate / Strong

**3. Workflow Integration Depth**
The most defensible AI products embed deeply into users' actual work — solving specific
problems in ways general-purpose tools cannot replicate. Deep workflow integration
creates switching costs that compound over time as users build processes around the tool.

Rate:
- **Surface** — tool sits alongside existing workflows, easy to swap out
- **Integrated** — connected to core systems (EHR, payer, billing), meaningful switching cost
- **Mission-critical** — deeply embedded in daily operations, high switching cost,
  workflow depends on the product's continued operation

**4. Feedback Loop Architecture**
Agentic AI systems become increasingly differentiated through use — they adapt to your
specific business context, learn your unique processes, and develop capabilities that
reflect your company's particular strengths. Generic AI applications tend to produce
similar results across different companies; agentic systems compound.

Rate:
- **None** — no learning from production usage
- **Passive** — usage data collected but not actively improving the product
- **Active flywheel** — more customers → more data → better model → more customers

**5. AI Cost Structure**
The commoditization of foundation models is shifting value away from model intelligence
toward infrastructure, distribution, and application-layer integration. Companies whose
differentiation relies on expensive proprietary inference may see margin pressure as
open-source models close the capability gap.

Evaluate:
- Is this company's value creation dependent on expensive inference, or does it survive
  model commoditization?
- Are there signals of usage-based pricing stress (high inference cost per transaction)?
- Does the business model improve as AI costs fall, or does it erode?

### The Thin Wrapper Trap
Startups that rapidly wrapped APIs in polished UIs experienced explosive early adoption
but are now vulnerable. The startups disrupted by model providers' native capabilities
were little more than applications waiting to be absorbed — similar to how third-party
spell checkers were absorbed by Microsoft Word. When evaluating competitors, flag
explicitly if they appear to be in this category.

### What Durable AI Moats Look Like in Healthcare/RCM
In regulated, accountability-heavy verticals like healthcare:
- Accountability creates a natural moat — industries that require accountability (legal,
  insurance, healthcare, defense) have higher barriers to entry even with AI, because
  the question of who is responsible for an AI decision cannot be automated away.
- Proprietary claims data, payer-specific outcomes data, and denial pattern data are
  highly defensible because they cannot be reconstructed after the fact
- EHR and payer integrations create deep workflow lock-in that pure-play AI competitors
  without those integrations cannot easily replicate
- Regulatory compliance requirements (HIPAA, payer credentialing) add friction that
  slows new entrants regardless of model quality

## Framework Selection Quick Reference

| Situation | Recommended Framework(s) |
|---|---|
| Mature market, structural analysis | Porter's Five Forces |
| Portfolio / investment posture | BCG Matrix + McKinsey 3 Horizons |
| Whitespace / differentiation | Blue Ocean Strategy |
| ICP-level value comparison | Value Proposition Canvas + JTBD |
| Business model / pricing | Business Model Canvas |
| Growth strategy prediction | Ansoff Matrix |
| Executive communication | Perceptual Map |
| Enterprise value drivers | Bain's Elements of Value |
| AI-native competitors present | AI Moat Framework (section 11) — layer on top of any of the above |
